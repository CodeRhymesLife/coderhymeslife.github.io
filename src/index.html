University of Washington PhD Candidate in Biomedical and Health Informatics

I'm researching ways to use medcial image processing, virtual reality (VR), mixed reality (MR), 3D modeling and 3D printing to decentralize catheterization procedrues.

Research Interests:
  • VR & MR Procedural Guidance: I'm investigating how VR and MR can improve catheterization procedures by comparing conventional procedures to those performed in a novel simulator (see medmod-sim under My GitHub above) using medical image processing, VR, MR, sensors and 3D printing (video 1, video 2).
  • VR and MR Surgical Planning and Education: How do VR and MR influence surgical planning and medical education? I'm exploring this question in novel VR and MR 3D model viewers (see medmod-vr and medmod-mr un My GitHub above) with physicians at Seattle Children's, UC Irvine and University of Washington (video)
  • Self Driving Catheters: I can't wait to explore how realtime ultrasound image processing, AI and robotics can programatically guide catheters. I hope this research helps improve who has access to safe catheter based treatments, because far too few do.

Healthcare Technology

Helping people with technology has always been an interest of mine, but when I helped a family understood their daughter's disease with a virtual reality application I built (video below) something in me changed. The idea that technology can help us understand what's inside us, improve peoples' access to care, and improve clinical outcomes is what excites me when I wake up. The mountains of underused and ignored, potentially life-saving data is what drives me to research novel ways to make data-driven decisions. My current work preserves the 3-dimensionality of data produced during cardiac catheterization procedures to better understand how virtual reality and mixed reality can facilitate minimally invasive therapies (videos below). In the near future, I hope to build on this work to understand how self driving catheters can address access to care problems.

Entrepreneurship

I plan to build a successful company that researches, develops and commercializes innovative healthcare technologies. Commercialization is important to me because it's a vehicle for bringing new ideas from the bench to bedside. I've learned many lessons over the years, including the importance of understanding market and stakeholders early. In 2016, I co-founded a company named Pear Med where we developed a virtual reality based surgical planning tool (video). We failed, in part, because we didn't understand our user's needs or how hospitals would purchase our product. I reflected on these important lessons before co-founding Pyrus, where we have an innovative solution for a large access to care problem.

At Pyrus we're developing a suitcase-sized electrophysiology lab that decentralizes cardiac ablations to bring safe procedures to rural areas where most atrial fibrillation (AFib) patients live. I couldn't be more excited about our mission and its potential impact. 33 million people suffer from AFib every year, yet only 5% of eligible patients receive the definitive treatment--cardiac ablation. This is an important issue to address because AFib can lead to stroke, heart attack and premature death, and it affects older adults--a population that will continue to grow as life expectancy increases. I look forward to updating this description as we learn from our market research.
